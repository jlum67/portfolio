Iterative Design | Lovecast


overview

The purpose of this group project was to:

  • Design an interactive interface for an emerging startup.
  • Gain insight into the full process of mocking up and designing a solution to the startup's concept.

We decided to create a mobile prototype for Lovecast, an app for celebrating events digitally. Users have the opportunity to interact and form memories with their guests, regardless of where they are in the world.

sketches

Together, we brainstormed a few different design options for the Lovecast app. We focused on three key pages of this app: the home page, livestream page, and invitations page. Here are some of the designs we came up with:


critiques

Before finalizing our hi-fi prototype, we received and made revisions based on the following critiques from our peers:

  • Increase the size of text, which could cause readibility issues for some users.
  • Increase the color contrast between the originally light pink button on top of the purple background.
  • Have the chat box in the livestream be collapsible.
  • Make the "Create Your Own Event" template stand out more.
  • Make the Pre-Stream Checklist icons more apparent that they are clickable.
  • Place the "Upload your own image" option before the built-in photos, since it makes more sense for users to add their own photos for personal events.
  • Include both a "Join" and "Details" button for events that are about to start, as opposed to just "Join."
  • Add clarity to the fact that more guests can be invited to an event.

prototype

Based on our sketches and the class critiques, we finalized a high fidelity prototype. While prototyping, we also kept in mind Lovecast's stated mission and goals, which we have listed below and described how we addressed them.

  • Goal #1: Host various events.
    • Addressed by: Having event templates on the home page to make creating events a seamless process for the user.
  • Goal #2: Join events from around the world.
    • Addressed by: A clearly laid out invitations page, and also featuring upcoming events on the home page for accessibility.
  • Goal #3: Make events interactive for all participants.
    • Addressed by: Livestream with chat box and emoticon reactions. We also considered the functionality of video streaming and included video settings, participant settings, etc.

Here is our high-fidelity prototype, made in Figma. Hope you enjoy!

user testing

After completing our high fidelity prototype, we went through a second round of feedback by submitting our updated prototype to UserTesting, where three testers would complete a task and provide feedback on how the process went for them.

  • Task #1: Start the tea party event.
    • None of the three testers had trouble finding the "Start" button and clicking the "Go Live" button. They all rated the task as 5 (very easy). This was in line with our expectations since we intentionally made the "Start" button for the upcoming events green and right below the event name so that it was easy for users to recognize and find.
  • Task #2: Check that the camera, audio, and connection settings work.
    • Users were able to successfully open the popup for each setting and also all rated the task as 5 (very easy). This matched our expectations since we had icons as buttons that represented each setting option, along with text labeling the setting so users would have a smooth experience understanding and adjusting the appropriate settings.
  • Task #3: End the livestream.
    • All users were able to quickly find the "Exit Stream" button and leave the page. All testers rated the task as 5 (very easy). This aligned with our expectations because we made the "Exit Stream" button red and at the top left of the screen, where people generally expect quit buttons, so they can easily exit the stream.

Based on our UserTesting results and feedback, we wouldn't change much about our interface, as all users gave positive feedback and didn't have any struggles. There was no unexpected behavior.

Below are the UserTesting videos, which show the users' interactions with our prototype.

conclusion and final thoughts

Designing the Lovecast app gave us the opportunity to not only explore various design choices, but also improve upon our first iteration of our prototype based on the feedback we received. We learned so much from this process, especially the importance of thoroughly testing a design and improving upon it.

Responsive Redesign


overview

The purpose of this project is to practice the workflow of redesigning a simple website. We achieve this by:

  • Analyzing and identifying flaws in an existing website.
  • Creating low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes for various screen sizes.
  • Building a responsive website based on research and prototypes.

identifying usability problems

Picking a webpage:

The webpage I chose to redesign is a Chinese government site that provides information about China's governmental structure, government news & communications, and services/programs available to non-residents.

Click here for link to selected webpage.



When I think about the purpose of a website, the first things that come to mind are transparency and accessibility to information and resources. This made me wonder whether different governments around the world — who should make information transparent and accessible to their citizens — designed websites with sufficient usability, learnability, and memorability. With this in mind, I researched different countries’ government websites, and arbitrarily came across China’s government website, which I thought had some room for improvement.

Finding problems with the webpage:

  • Lack of hierarchy, since section headings use different fonts, colors, and backgrounds.
  • Lack of sufficient text and background contrast in some areas, making content difficult to read.
  • No clear form of navigating through the site. Though there are navigation bar-like components on the left and right side of the screen, the user must scroll through the page to see what options are available to them.
  • Interactive elements (hovering and clicking) are not consistent throughout the page. Some items react with underline when hovering, while others have no response at all.
  • Users are not aware of system status, since clicking buttons sometimes has no response.
  • Site is not responsive on different devices. The whole screen just becomes smaller, making the font hard to read and the site even harder to navigate.
  • Very text heavy and has irrelevant information for a home page, which "hides" relevant content and makes the site overwhelming for users.
  • Sections of site are sometimes left unlabeled, leaving users confused as to what they are looking at.
  • Lack of logo or site title makes user unsure of what site they are even looking at.

Accessibility:

WebAIM WAVE was used to detect possible accessibility problems with the site. and my main takeaways from the evaluation were that:

  • There was no heading structure.
  • No page regions or ARIA landmarks were found.
  • The language of the site was not identified with a lang attribute.
  • Layout tables were used, which create reading and navigation order issues for screen readers.
  • No alternative text was used for images, which makes them "invisible" to screen readers, and users when an image is unavailable.
  • Low contrast between text and background.

I definitely agree with the problems detected, particularly the issues with no heading structure and low text-background contrast. In my initial analysis of the site, I could not recognize different sections of the page since inconsistent headings, color, and fonts were used for content that should have the same hierarchy level. In addition, I also found some text difficult to read due to the background color and lack of contrast. The other accessibility problems that the evaluation found aren't visible upon looking at the site, but I can understand why they create barriers to accessing information for screen readers.

High-fidelity Prototyping:

Based on my low-fidelity wireframes, I created 3 high-fidelity prototypes, one for each type of device (computer, tablet, and phone). Each prototype is annotated with the different elements that a developer would need to reproduce my website. Note that many components from the computer prototype are duplicated, so annotations made in that prototype apply to the others as well.

Before you view the link to my prototypes below, I have a few general annotations to mention here that apply to my entire redesign. If you are familiar with bootstrap's heading library and grid system, then you can skip the collapsible component below. If you aren't familiar (like I was before this project!), then please open the collapsible component and keep reading.

Most of my text is sized using bootstrap's heading library, which has sizes h1 to h6 (h1 is largest, h6 is smallest).

In addition to divs, flex boxes, and floating elements, the responsiveness of my page to different devices is done using the bootstrap grid system. This grid system essentially divides the page into 12 columns. In my code, I can define how many columns each section of my site takes up, and explicitly code how this should change based on the screen size. I can also define the order of the divs based on the size of the screen. For the grid system, I split my entire site into 2 sections/divs. One includes the News and Resources components, and the other includes the Most Requested components.

  • When the device being used is large (computers), I have the news/resources div take up 7 columns and the most requested div take up 5 columns. I have the news/resources div come first (which is why it's on the left side for larger screens).
  • When the device being used is medium-sized (tablets) or small (phones), I have the news/resources div take up 12 columns and the most requested div take up 12 columns. This basically means that they will be stacked on top of each other. For medium and small screens, I have the most requested div come first (which is why it appears on top in smaller screens).

For the bootstrap grid, my News and Resources div has the following code in its "class" field: col-sm-12 col-md-12 col-lg-7 order-sm-2 order-md-2 order-lg-1.

For the bootstrap grid, my Most Requested div has the following code in its "class" field: col-sm-12 col-md-12 col-lg-5 order-first order-md-1 order-lg-2.

Personas and Storyboarding


overview

The purpose of this project is to observe real users interacting with an interface, interview these individuals about their experiences, create personas based on these users, and illustrate a storyboard for one of their personas.

My chosen interface to examine is Brown's snack vending machines. What are students' positive and negative experiences with the vending machine interface, and why do they choose to use them over nearby dining halls?

preparation

Prepared questions for users:

  • When you walk by the vending machine, do you stop to take a look at the selection of items? What do you do if you see something appealing?
  • What frustrations and challenges do you experience in the process of selecting a product from the vending machine? Is the interface easy to use and understand?
  • What frustrations and challenges do you experience in the payment process of the vending machine? Is the interface easy to use and understand?
  • What emotions or thoughts come to mind while you are in each step of the purchase process (deciding on food, selecting desired product, and paying)?
  • Are there any times where your experience with the vending machine deviated from your expected experience?

Interface:

This is a sketch of the vending machine and its payment/food selection interface. Users use the pin pad to select their desired food item (which is labeled by number in the machine), and then swipe their card (credit or Brown ID) or insert cash to pay. Each of the machine's key interactive components are shown and described in the drawing.

recording observations

Key Observations from Observing Users:

  • Some people glance at the machine as they walk by, or pause briefly to see the selection of items.
  • Other people approach the machine directly and scan through the rows and columns of items.
  • If people are in a group, they point to different items.
  • While looking through the items, some users touched their chins or scratched their heads.
  • Nearly all users punched in numbers to see the cost of different items.
  • Some users glanced behind themselves, and tapped their feet while their payment was processing especially if there was someone behind them waiting to use the machine.
  • All users either used their Brown ID or credit card.

Summary of User Interview Responses:

  • Vending machine takes more money than is necessary for the desired product, and sometimes "eats" your money if your product gets stuck in the dispensing process.
  • Needing to use the pin pad to figure out the cost for every item is tedious. It would be nice if prices for all items were visible at once.
  • Card swipe takes a long time to process, and it is also frustrating that people can take money from your card if you forget to press "done" after purchasing your item.
  • The selection of items rotates frequently, so there is no for sure way to know what items will be available.
  • Nutritional facts and calories of products are not always visible.

personas

In this section, I develop personas for two users based on my previous observations and interviews. But, what exactly is a persona? Personas are used to represent overarching trends observed in users, and each persona represents one archetypal character on the spectrum of users. We achieve this by describing what users think, feel, say, and do.



Busy Benjamin:

Ben is a premed student at Brown struggling to manage his heavy course load in chemistry and biology classes, while also being involved in lab research and pre-professional clubs.

Ben represents vending machine users who are in a rush and, upon passing a vending machine, decide to get a quick snack to sustain themselves. His course load and struggles with procrastination lead him to complete assignments right before they are due, so he lacks time to walk to a dining hall before his next commitment. His interface problems mainly revolve around the payment system. He is frustrated by the long delay after swiping his card and the need to press "complete" to ensure someone else doesn't use his flex points. All of this is especially more frustrating since he uses vending machines when in a rush.




Athlete Ava:

Ava is a student-athlete at Brown studying cognitive science.

She represents consistent vending machine users who make intentional trips to purchase food (rather than walking by and then deciding to purchase something) and rely on vending machines for nighttime snacks. As a student-athlete, Ava requires protein snacks after her practices, but her practices end late at night so none of her nearby dining halls are open. Vending machines meet this gap for her. Since Ava is a consistent user, she has more experience with the frustrations of using the vending machine, and the lower-probability events of payment not working or food getting stuck are more familiar to her . Her interface problems include:

  • The inconsistency of vending machine selections, leading her to be unable to anticipate if her desired items are available.
  • The inability to see the nutritional contents of the food she's eating, which is important to her since she frequently exercises and needs to properly recuperate.
  • Flex points sometimes not being accepted. However, since she relies on the vending machine, she has no choice but to use a credit card (which also represents another group of users).
  • The length/delayed response of the payment system, and the anxiety that comes when other people also have to use the machine.
  • The annoyance of having to punch in every item's number to see the price.
  • Food getting stuck.

storyboard for busy benjamin

Storyboards use images and brief descriptions to reflect the goals of a user persona and include all necessary steps for using an interface.

conclusion and final thoughts

Working on this project was super interesting, as I gained more insight into how to effectively perform user interviews as well as visually represent my user research and observations! Generally, I think people find that the vending machine interface makes sense and matches the users' mental model. However, there are different aspects of the process that could be smoothened and made more efficient to increase overall user satisfaction.

A/B Testing and Eye Tracking


overview

The purpose of this project was to explore eye tracking, a common form of evaluating the performance of a page. Eye tracking provides valuable qualitative data for improving usability and increasing how efficiently our site guides users.

This project included the following components:

  • Develop two different prototypes of a bakery site.
  • Ideate a hypothesis based on the two different prototypes. How will their differences affect where a user’s eyes are drawn?
  • Conduct eye tracking tests with test participants.
  • Based on the eye tracking tests, generate heatmaps (a still image that depicts where participants’ gazes were fixed).
  • Interpret the results of the eye tracking and heatmap generation. Did our results match our initial hypothesis?

prototyping and hypothesis

Here are the two different designs we came up with for our bakery website.

Our Hypothesis:

We hypothesize that in both of our versions, a user’s eyes will scan left to right because we have created separate rows for pastries, cakes, and drinks that are each different colors.

For the prototype on the left, we hypothesize that users will look toward the quantity buttons and shopping cart icon, in order to determine how many items are in their carts. And, for the prototype on the right, we hypothesize that users will be drawn to look toward the shopping cart.

eye tracking

After developing our prototypes, we found two random test participants to try out our prototypes. They were given the task of browsing through the items, adding to the cart, and checking out.

Using eye tracking technology from Brown University's Human-Computer Interaction Research lab, we gathered qualitative data which showed us where on our prototypes users' eyes were drawn to. This data allows us to create heatmaps and map out their gaze motion.

conclusions

For prototype 1, our results mostly matched our expectations, as it showed that the user looked at the shopping cart icon and the quantity buttons often, and also the user’s eyes scanned left to right in the way we hypothesized (but then became a little more scattered halfway through the scan, not as much as in prototype 2 though).

For prototype 2, our results did not match our expectations, as it showed that the user barely looked at the shopping cart (probably because it’s not interactive and nothing in the cart updated) and they also did not look at the selections in an orderly fashion: they jumped from middle to top to bottom randomly.
